Grieving a Relationship That Wasn't "That Serious"

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published April 25, 2026 · Grief & Loss

Three months. A situationship that never got officially defined. A relationship you talked yourself out of taking seriously because it "wasn't that deep." And yet here you are, grieving it in a way that feels disproportionate to how you've described it to your friends, or even to yourself.

Grief Isn't Measured by Duration

The intensity of grief has much more to do with what the relationship represented than how long it lasted on a calendar. A short relationship that reactivated an old attachment wound, or that represented a hope you'd been holding onto for a long time, can produce more grief than a longer relationship that had quietly been fading for months before it ended.

Why You Might Be Minimizing It

A lot of people preemptively downplay a "shorter" or undefined relationship to avoid feeling embarrassed about how much it hurts. But minimizing the loss doesn't make the grief smaller — it just adds shame on top of it, and makes it harder to get support, because you've already told everyone, including yourself, that it wasn't a big deal.

What You're Actually Grieving

Often, what's being grieved isn't just the person — it's the version of a future you'd let yourself imagine, the hope that this might have been different, or the confirmation of a fear that good things don't last for you. None of that requires a long relationship to be real. It just requires that you cared, and that you're human.

Giving Yourself Permission

You don't need anyone else's validation that this loss is significant enough to grieve properly. If it's affecting your sleep, your mood, or your ability to focus, it's real enough to take seriously — regardless of how you'd summarize the relationship in one sentence to a friend.
