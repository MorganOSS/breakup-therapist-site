What Is IFS Therapy? A Beginner's Guide to Healing After Heartbreak

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published March 8, 2026 · IFS Therapy

If you've never heard of Internal Family Systems, or IFS, the name alone doesn't explain much. So here's the plain-English version: IFS is a therapy model built on the idea that your mind isn't one single voice reacting to life. It's made up of different "parts" — each with its own job, its own fears, and its own way of trying to protect you — plus a core Self underneath all of them.

Why This Matters After a Breakup

Heartbreak is one of the clearest places to see this in action. One part of you might be furious at your ex. Another part is still hoping they'll text. Another part feels numb and just wants to sleep for three days. A part might be deeply ashamed that you're taking this so hard. None of these are separate people inside you — they're all parts of you, showing up at once, often contradicting each other completely.

The Core Idea: Parts Aren't the Problem

Most approaches to mental health treat symptoms like anxiety or anger as problems to eliminate. IFS treats them as protectors — parts of you trying to keep you safe, usually based on strategies that made sense a long time ago, sometimes long before this relationship existed. The anxious part checking your phone every ten minutes isn't broken. It's trying to prevent another blindside. The numb part isn't lazy. It's protecting you from feeling all of the loss at once.

What Healing Actually Looks Like

In IFS work, healing doesn't mean silencing these parts or willing yourself to "get over it faster." It means getting to know them: understanding what each one is afraid of, what it needs, and helping it trust that you — your core Self — can actually handle what's happening now. Over time, the parts stop needing to shout so loudly, because they trust that someone is finally listening.

What This Looks Like In Session

Practically, this means we don't just talk about your breakup in the abstract. We get curious about specific moments — the urge to check their location, the wave of rage that surprised you, the flat numbness on a Tuesday morning — and ask what part of you that is, and what it's trying to do for you. It's slower than a quick coping-skills approach, but it goes somewhere a coping skill can't: the actual architecture of why you feel what you feel.

If this is the first time you've heard of IFS, that's completely normal — most people haven't, until they need it. If it resonates, that's usually a sign it's worth exploring further.
