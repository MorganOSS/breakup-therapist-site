Anxious Attachment

How to Stop Chasing Reassurance After a Relationship Ends

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published April 9, 2026 · Anxious Attachment

After a breakup, reassurance-seeking can take a lot of forms: texting your ex under a practical excuse, asking friends for the tenth time whether you did the right thing, replaying the breakup conversation looking for a detail that changes the meaning. Each one offers a brief hit of relief, followed by the same anxiety, needing to be soothed again shortly after.

Why Reassurance Never Actually Satisfies

Reassurance-seeking works like scratching an itch that's actually a rash — it feels better for a second and then makes the underlying irritation worse. That's because the anxious part driving the search isn't actually looking for one piece of information; it's looking for a guarantee that you'll be okay, and no single answer can ever fully provide that, so the search starts again almost immediately.

The Difference Between Support and Reassurance-Seeking

There's a real difference between reaching out to a friend for genuine support and reaching out repeatedly for the same reassurance in slightly different words. Support helps you process a feeling. Reassurance-seeking is often an attempt to make an uncomfortable feeling disappear entirely, which isn't actually possible, and chasing it tends to keep the anxious part more activated, not less.

What Helps Instead

Rather than fighting the urge outright, try naming it when it shows up: "this is the part of me that wants certainty it can't have right now." Then ask what you actually need in that moment — often it's not information about your ex at all, but comfort, distraction, or simply acknowledgment that this is hard. Giving yourself that directly, instead of routing it through a search for reassurance, tends to work better and doesn't come with the same rebound anxiety.

Breaking this cycle isn't about willpower alone — it's about understanding what the anxious part is actually afraid of, and helping it trust that you can tolerate uncertainty without a guarantee attached to it.
