Therapy Directories vs. Personal Referrals: Why Fit Matters More Than Credentials

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published July 10, 2026 · Local & Practical

Therapist directories are useful for one thing: confirming that someone is licensed and lists relevant specialties. What they can't tell you is whether you'll feel understood by that specific person, and that gap is exactly where a lot of people end up with a technically qualified therapist who still isn't the right fit.

Why Credentials Are a Floor, Not a Ceiling

Licensure and specialty keywords tell you someone meets a minimum professional standard. They don't tell you how someone actually communicates, whether their pacing matches what you need, or whether they'll understand the specific texture of your situation — a queer breakup, a divorce with complicated family dynamics, an anxious attachment pattern that's shown up in every relationship you've had.

Why Personal Referrals Tend to Work Better

A referral from someone who actually knows both you and the therapist landscape can account for things a directory search can't: communication style, therapeutic approach, and specific lived experience with situations like yours. That's a fundamentally different kind of matching than filtering a directory by zip code and specialty tags.

The Cost of Getting the Match Wrong

A poor-fit therapist doesn't just waste time — it can actively discourage people from trying therapy again at all, especially if they interpret a bad match as evidence that therapy itself doesn't work for them, rather than evidence that this specific pairing wasn't right.

Why I Built a Referral Service Around This Exact Problem

This gap between credentials and actual fit is part of why I started offering a personal therapist-matching service alongside my own practice — meeting with people directly, understanding their specific situation, and making real recommendations rather than pointing them toward a directory search to do alone.

If you've tried a directory search and it hasn't led anywhere useful, a more personal approach to finding the right fit might be exactly what's missing.
