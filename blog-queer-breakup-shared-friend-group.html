Queer Breakups and Community: When Your Ex Is Still in Your Friend Group

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published May 31, 2026 · LGBTQ+

In a lot of breakup advice, "cut contact and give yourself space" is treated as the default recommendation. For queer folks in smaller or tight-knit community spaces, that advice often doesn't map onto reality. Your ex might still be at the same events, part of the same friend group, someone you'll see regularly whether you plan to or not.

Why This Is a Distinct Challenge

Queer community, especially somewhere like Utah where it can be smaller and more interconnected than in bigger cities, often functions as chosen family — which means a breakup doesn't just end a relationship, it potentially destabilizes your whole social support system at exactly the moment you need it most. That's a much higher-stakes version of "it's complicated" than most breakup advice accounts for.

The Pressure to Keep Things Smooth

There's often unspoken pressure to manage the breakup gracefully for the sake of the group — to not make things awkward, to not force friends to pick sides, to perform more okay-ness than you actually feel in shared spaces. That pressure is real, and it's also exhausting, especially layered on top of an already painful breakup.

What Actually Helps

Rather than pretending a clean break is possible, it helps to get specific: which events can you skip for a while without real cost, where do you actually need a strategy for coexisting, and where can you be honest with mutual friends about needing a little grace right now. You don't have to solve the whole community dynamic at once — just the next event, the next interaction.

Give Yourself Permission to Grieve the Complexity, Too

Part of what makes this hard is grieving not just the relationship, but the loss of the simplicity you might have had if you could just avoid your ex entirely. That's a real loss worth naming, not a minor inconvenience layered on top of the "real" grief.

If navigating a breakup inside a shared queer community feels like its own particular kind of hard, that's because it is — and it's exactly the kind of nuance affirming therapy is built to hold.
