Why Breakup Grief Doesn't Follow the Five Stages

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published April 21, 2026 · Grief & Loss

Most people know the five stages of grief — denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance — and try to map their breakup onto them, waiting for the stages to arrive in order and eventually resolve into acceptance. If your grief hasn't followed that pattern, here's some relief: it was never designed to.

Where the Five Stages Actually Came From

The model was developed by Elisabeth Kübler-Ross to describe the experience of terminally ill patients facing their own death, not a general framework for all loss, and definitely not for the specific, ongoing, often ambiguous loss of a breakup. Applying it directly to heartbreak sets up an expectation the model was never built to meet.

Why Breakup Grief Looks Different

Breakup grief is complicated by things death grief usually isn't: the person is often still alive, sometimes still in your social circle, sometimes still texting you occasionally. There's no finality to grieve into, which means "acceptance" as a clean endpoint doesn't quite apply the same way. Breakup grief also frequently involves grieving a future you'd imagined, not just a person, which adds a layer the five-stage model doesn't address at all.

What Breakup Grief Actually Looks Like

In practice, it tends to move in waves rather than stages — good days followed by a hard day for no obvious reason, moments of feeling fully recovered followed by a memory that flattens you again. It isn't linear, and it doesn't reliably get smaller in a straight line. It tends to get less frequent and less intense over time, but rarely in the tidy progression the five stages suggest.

Why This Reframe Matters

If you're measuring your healing against a model that doesn't fit, you'll likely conclude you're doing it wrong, even when you're not. Letting go of the five-stage expectation tends to relieve a layer of shame that has nothing to do with the actual breakup, and everything to do with a mismatched framework.

Real breakup grief work meets you in the actual, messy, wave-like shape of your experience — not the shape a model built for something else predicted you'd have.
