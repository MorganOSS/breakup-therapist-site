Anxious Attachment

Why Anxious Attachment Makes Breakups Hurt So Much More

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published April 17, 2026 · Anxious Attachment

Two people can go through what looks like a similar breakup and have completely different experiences of it. One person is sad for a few weeks and moves forward. The other feels like their whole foundation has collapsed. If you tend to be the second person, and it's happened more than once, anxious attachment is often a meaningful part of the explanation.

It's Not About How "Serious" the Relationship Was

People with anxious attachment often assume the intensity of their pain means the relationship must have been unusually significant, or that something is wrong with them for hurting this much over something that "wasn't that serious." In reality, the intensity has more to do with how the ending is being processed internally than with the objective size of the relationship.

Why the Loss Feels Like a Threat, Not Just Sadness

For anxious attachment, a breakup doesn't just mean losing a person — it can activate a deeper fear about your own worth and safety in the world. The nervous system doesn't just register "this relationship ended"; it registers something closer to "I am now unsafe and unlovable," which is a much bigger, scarier message than grief alone.

Why This Isn't Overreacting

None of this means you're being dramatic or overreacting. It means your nervous system is responding to an old pattern of fear about abandonment, layered on top of the real and valid grief of losing someone. Both things are true at once: the relationship mattered, and the intensity of your reaction is also being amplified by attachment history that predates this relationship entirely.

What Helps Close the Gap

Working through anxious attachment directly — understanding where the fear of abandonment originated and building a more secure relationship with yourself — tends to reduce how catastrophic future breakups feel, even when the grief itself is still real. The goal isn't to feel less about people who matter to you. It's to stop every ending feeling like evidence that you're fundamentally unsafe.
