Your Most Important Relationship Is The One You Have With Yourself.

Everything else — who you date, how you grieve, how fast you heal — grows out of that one.

Why This Hurts So Much

Breakups don't just end a relationship — they end a version of your future you'd already started living in. That's part of why the pain can feel disproportionate to what other people expect from you, or what you expect from yourself.

I work through a lens called Internal Family Systems, or IFS. The idea is simple: you're not one single self reacting to heartbreak. You're made up of many parts — a part that's furious, a part that's still hoping for a text back, a part that's ashamed of how hard this is hitting you, and a part underneath all of it that just wants to feel safe again.

When a relationship ends, these parts don't take turns politely. They all show up at once, which is why you can feel wildly anxious and completely numb in the same afternoon. None of those parts are the problem. They're all trying to protect you in the only way they know how — usually a way that made sense a long time ago, before this relationship, before this pattern.

Healing isn't about silencing those parts or willing yourself to "get over it faster." It's about getting to know them — understanding what each one is afraid of, and what each one needs — until they can trust that you've got this, and start to soften. That's the work we'll do together.
