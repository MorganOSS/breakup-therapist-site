Why "Just Get Over It" Doesn't Work (An IFS Perspective)

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published March 20, 2026 · IFS Therapy

Somewhere in the first few weeks after a breakup, someone well-meaning will tell you to just get over it, move on, or stop giving them so much space in your head. It's usually said with love. It's also almost never helpful, and there's a real reason why, beyond just "healing takes time."

What "Just Get Over It" Actually Asks You To Do

That advice assumes healing is a decision — that you can simply choose to stop feeling what you're feeling. But in Internal Family Systems terms, the parts of you that are grieving, obsessing, or aching aren't being stubborn for no reason. They're doing a job: processing a real loss, protecting you from repeating a painful pattern, or holding onto hope because letting go feels too dangerous right now. Telling those parts to just stop is like telling a smoke alarm to stop going off without checking if there's a fire.

Why This Advice Backfires

When you try to force a part to be quiet, it usually gets louder, not softer — the same way a thought you try hard not to think gets harder to stop thinking. Worse, "just get over it" often adds a second layer of pain: now you're not just hurting, you're also ashamed that you haven't stopped hurting on someone else's timeline. That shame becomes its own part to manage, on top of everything else.

What Actually Works Instead

Rather than fighting the parts that are struggling, IFS work asks a different question: what does this part need in order to feel safe enough to soften? Usually it's not less attention — it's more, just directed differently. The anxious part needs to be heard, not silenced. The grieving part needs space, not a deadline. Counterintuitively, turning toward these parts with curiosity tends to move things forward faster than trying to muscle past them ever does.

If you've been trying to will yourself out of heartbreak and it's not working, that's not a failure of effort. It's a sign the parts involved need a different kind of attention than pure willpower can offer.
