Meet Your Parts: Why You Can't Stop Checking Their Instagram

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published March 12, 2026 · IFS Therapy

You know it doesn't help. You've told yourself a hundred times you're going to stop. And yet, somewhere between a boring meeting and a quiet drive home, you're back on their profile, scanning for clues about how they're doing without you. If this sounds familiar, you're not weak-willed. You're dealing with a part of you that has a very specific job, and it's not going to quit that job just because your rational mind told it to.

What That Part Is Actually Doing

In Internal Family Systems terms, the part of you that checks their Instagram is usually a protector — trying to gather information so you're never blindsided again. Maybe it's watching for signs they've moved on, so you can brace yourself before you hear it secondhand. Maybe it's looking for proof they're struggling too, so the breakup feels more even. Maybe it's just trying to stay connected to them in the only way it still can. None of those are irrational goals. They're just being pursued through a channel that keeps the wound open.

Why Willpower Doesn't Work Here

Telling yourself to "just stop" treats this like a discipline problem. But this part isn't undisciplined — it's scared, and it doesn't yet trust that you can handle uncertainty without the information it's trying to gather. Fighting it head-on usually backfires, because parts respond to being shamed the same way people do: they dig in harder.

What Actually Helps

Instead of white-knuckling past the urge, try getting curious about it in the moment. Ask: what is this part afraid will happen if I don't check? Usually the answer reveals something real — a fear of being replaced, a fear of not knowing, a fear of being the only one still hurting. Naming that fear directly, even just to yourself, often does more than any app-blocker ever could, because you're finally addressing what the part actually needs instead of fighting the behavior on the surface.

This is the kind of pattern that responds well to IFS work specifically, because it's not really about social media at all — it's about what that checking is trying to protect you from feeling.
