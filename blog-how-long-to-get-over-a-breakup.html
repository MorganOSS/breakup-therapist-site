How Long Does It Take to Get Over a Breakup? A Therapist's Honest Answer

← Back to the Blog
Rose Andre
Published June 8, 2026 · Breakup Psychology

There's a popular formula floating around that says it takes half the length of the relationship to get over a breakup. It's a tidy idea, and it's not actually true. Recovery time depends on far more than how long you were together, and the honest answer is less satisfying than a formula, but far more useful.

What Actually Determines Recovery Time

How attached you were, how the relationship ended, whether it activated older attachment wounds, how much support you have, and whether you're actively processing the loss or avoiding it all matter more than the calendar length of the relationship. A short relationship that reopened an old wound can take longer to move through than a longer relationship that had been quietly ending for a while before it officially did.

Why "Getting Over It" Isn't Really the Goal

Framing recovery as "getting over" someone implies a finish line where the relationship stops mattering entirely. In practice, healing tends to look more like the relationship taking up less space and less urgency over time, rather than disappearing completely. You can be fully healed and still have a memory that brings up a flicker of something when it surfaces unexpectedly.

Signs You're Actually Healing, Even Without a Timeline

Rather than counting weeks, better indicators include: thinking about them less compulsively, feeling more curious about your own life again, being able to recall the relationship without it derailing your whole day, and finding that hard days are further apart and shorter than they used to be.

When Recovery Feels Stuck

If real time has passed and none of those markers are shifting at all, that's often a sign the grief needs more active support, not more passive waiting. Time helps, but time alone doesn't automatically process everything — sometimes it just delays dealing with what actually needs attention.

There's no universal number of weeks or months that applies to everyone. There is a real difference between healing that's actively happening, even slowly, and healing that's stalled — and that difference is usually more useful to track than the calendar.
